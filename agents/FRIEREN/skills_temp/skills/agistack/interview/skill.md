# Interview

## What the Interviewer Is Actually Thinking

There is a version of interview preparation that focuses entirely on the candidate — what to say, how to say it, which stories to tell, which weaknesses to mention that are secretly strengths. This version misses something important.

The interviewer is not a neutral evaluator administering an objective assessment. They are a person with a problem. They have a role that is open, which means work that is not getting done or getting done by people who are already stretched. They have spent time sourcing, screening, and scheduling. They are hoping, genuinely hoping, that the person sitting across from them is the person who makes this problem go away.

They are also carrying risk. A bad hire reflects on them. The candidate who seemed impressive in the interview and struggled in the role is a story that follows the interviewer. They are not just evaluating whether you can do the job. They are evaluating whether they can defend the decision to hire you if it does not work out.

Understanding this changes how you prepare. You are not performing for a judge. You are solving a problem for someone who wants you to succeed at solving it.

## The Research That Changes Everything

Most candidates research a company the way they research a restaurant — enough to know roughly what it is and whether they want to go. They read the About page, skim the recent news, and arrive with a general sense of what the company does.

This is the floor, not the ceiling.

The research that changes an interview outcome is specific enough to be useful in the conversation. Not "I know you recently expanded to Europe" but "I noticed the European expansion happened alongside the Series B, which suggests the thesis is about market share before profitability — is that still the operating assumption, or has the thinking evolved?" Not "I'm excited about your culture" but "I read the post your CTO wrote about engineering culture last year and I had a reaction to the part about how you handle technical debt — can I share it?"

This level of specificity communicates preparation, genuine interest, and the ability to engage at the level the role requires. It also generates conversations rather than answers, which is where real impressions are formed.

The skill builds a complete research brief for any company and role. What the company does and how it makes money. The recent news that is worth knowing and worth asking about. The leadership team and what they have said publicly about where the company is going. The competitive landscape and the challenges the company is navigating. The specific role and what success in it probably looks like given everything else you know.

## Your Stories Are Your Evidence

Interviews are won and lost on stories. Not on qualifications — qualifications get you in the room. On the specific, concrete, detailed accounts of what you have actually done under real conditions with real stakes.

Behavioral interview questions — tell me about a time when, give me an example of, describe a situation where — exist because past behavior under real pressure is more predictive of future performance than any answer to a hypothetical. The interviewer is not interested in what you would do. They are interested in what you did, because what you did is evidence and what you would do is conjecture.

The skill helps you build a story library from your actual experience. It works through your background and identifies the moments that contain the evidence most relevant to the roles you are pursuing — the project that went wrong and how you handled it, the decision you made with incomplete information, the time you disagreed with a direction and what you did about it, the result you are most proud of and why it was hard to produce.

For each story it helps you structure the account in a way that is specific enough to be believable, concise enough to be listenable, and complete enough to answer the question that was actually asked rather than a slightly different question that was easier to answer.

## The Questions You Are Not Prepared For

Every interview contains at least one question you did not expect. The one that arrives from a direction you were not watching, about something you had not thought to prepare, at a moment when the conversation had been going well enough that the surprise lands harder.

The skill prepares you for the questions specific to your background and the specific role. The gap in your resume that will be noticed. The job you left after eight months that will be asked about. The skill listed on your resume that you are less confident in than the others. The transition in your career that does not follow an obvious logic. The failure that is visible in the record if someone looks carefully.

For each one it helps you prepare an answer that is honest, specific, and forward-looking — that acknowledges what is real without dwelling on it, that explains the context without making excuses, that demonstrates the self-awareness that makes the concern less concerning rather than more.

## Mock Interviews That Tell the Truth

There is a version of mock interview practice that feels useful and is not. A friend asks you questions. You answer them. They nod. You feel more prepared.

Useful practice requires honest feedback, which requires someone willing to tell you that the answer was too long, that you used a filler word eleven times, that the story you told did not actually answer the question that was asked, that you looked away at the moment when eye contact would have mattered most.

The skill runs mock interviews and provides this kind of feedback. It asks the questions an interviewer at your target company and level would ask, in the order and with the follow-ups that real interviews contain. After each answer it tells you specifically what worked, what did not, and what a stronger version of the answer would have included.

It tracks patterns across multiple practice sessions — the tendency to over-explain, the habit of qualifying strong statements until they become ambiguous, the stories you reach for reflexively that are not always the best evidence for the point you are making. These patterns, identified before the real interview, can be addressed. Identified after, they are missed opportunities.

## The Salary Conversation

The salary conversation in an interview process is a negotiation, and like all negotiations it is easier for the party that has more information and has thought more carefully about their position before the conversation begins.

The skill prepares you for every version of this conversation. The early-stage question about salary expectations before an offer exists, asked to establish a range before the company has revealed its own. The offer that arrives at the bottom of the range you discussed. The response to your counter that says the budget is firm. The silence after you name your number.

For each moment it provides specific language — not scripts to read but words you can make your own, calibrated to the relationship you have built and the leverage you actually have. It helps you understand your market value specifically enough to anchor to a number with confidence rather than a range with apology.

## The Thank You That Is Not a Formality

The follow-up message after an interview is treated by most candidates as a courtesy — a brief note sent within twenty-four hours that thanks the interviewer for their time and reiterates interest.

This is a missed opportunity.

A follow-up that references something specific from the conversation — a concern raised that you have thought more about since leaving, a question asked that you want to answer more completely, a point made by the interviewer that changed how you are thinking about the role — communicates something that the interview itself could not fully demonstrate: that you were genuinely present, that you are still thinking, that the conversation mattered enough to continue.

The skill drafts this follow-up immediately after the interview while the specifics are still clear. It captures what was discussed, what was left unresolved, and what the message should accomplish — moving you forward rather than merely maintaining your position.

## When You Do Not Get the Offer

A rejection from a process you invested in seriously is genuinely disappointing. It is also, if handled correctly, an opportunity.

The company that rejected you knows your work. The interviewer who liked you but could not advocate successfully for you may be at a different company in a year. The role that was not right for you this time may open again when the fit is better. The feedback from the rejection, if you ask for it professionally and receive it honestly, may be the most direct information you have ever gotten about how you are perceived by the market.

The skill helps you close the loop on a rejection in a way that leaves every door open. The message that thanks the team, asks a specific question about what would have made you a stronger candidate, and expresses genuine interest in staying connected. The response that treats the feedback as information rather than verdict. The follow-up six months later that demonstrates you took the feedback seriously.

The candidate who handles rejection with grace is remembered differently than the one who disappears. That difference occasionally produces an offer that the rejection seemed to foreclose.
