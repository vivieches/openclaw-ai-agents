# Recruiting

## The Most Expensive Mistake You Make Slowly

A bad hire does not announce itself on day one. It reveals itself over weeks and months — in work that needs to be redone, in team friction that did not exist before, in the manager's time consumed by a performance situation that everyone saw coming and nobody addressed early enough. By the time the cost is visible, it has already compounded.

Research on the cost of a bad hire consistently produces numbers that seem too high until you do the arithmetic yourself. Recruiting fees or time spent sourcing. Salary paid during a tenure that produced little. The productivity of the manager who hired them, consumed by managing the situation. The morale cost to the team that watched the situation unfold. The opportunity cost of the role that was not filled with someone who would have made the team stronger.

The number is not small. And the mistake that produced it was almost never made at the offer stage. It was made earlier — in a job description that attracted the wrong people, in an interview process that asked the wrong questions, in a decision made on gut feeling rather than evidence, in a standard that was quietly lowered because the process had taken too long and everyone was tired.

This skill builds the process that prevents the mistake.

## The Job Description Is a Filter

Most job descriptions are written to describe a job. The best ones are written to attract a specific person and repel everyone else.

A job description that tries to appeal to everyone attracts applications from people who apply to everything. The screening process then does the work of narrowing the field that the job description should have done before anyone applied. This is backwards and expensive.

The skill writes job descriptions that function as filters. They are specific about what the role actually requires — not an aspirational list of every skill that would be nice to have, but a clear articulation of the two or three things that will determine whether this person succeeds or fails in this role. They describe the work honestly, including the parts that are hard, because candidates who are deterred by an honest description of the challenges are candidates who would have left anyway. They are written in the language of the people you want to attract, not the language of HR compliance.

The result is fewer applications and better ones — which is always the right trade.

## Screening That Is Actually Predictive

A resume tells you what someone has done. It tells you almost nothing about whether they can do what you need them to do, in your specific environment, at the level you require.

The screening step exists to bridge that gap — to identify, from the evidence available before an interview, which candidates are worth investing interview time in and which are not. Done poorly, screening is pattern matching against credentials that correlate loosely with success. Done well, it is a structured assessment of the specific evidence that predicts performance in this role.

The skill evaluates candidates against the criteria that actually matter for the role you defined. For each candidate it produces a match assessment covering the genuine strengths relative to the role requirements, the gaps or concerns that warrant probing in the interview, and a recommended next step. The assessment is specific enough to be useful and honest enough to be trusted.

## Interviews That Reveal Rather Than Confirm

Most interviews confirm what the interviewer already believes. A candidate who presents well in the first five minutes is asked questions that invite them to elaborate on their strengths. A candidate who presents less well is asked questions that inadvertently surface their weaknesses. The interview produces evidence for a conclusion that was already forming rather than genuinely testing it.

Structured interviews are significantly more predictive than unstructured ones. Not because the questions are harder, but because every candidate is assessed against the same criteria using the same questions, which means the comparison is meaningful rather than contaminated by irrelevant variation in how the conversation happened to go.

The skill generates structured interview question sets for any role. Behavioral questions that ask for specific past examples rather than hypothetical responses, because past behavior under real pressure is more predictive than imagined behavior under no pressure. Technical or functional questions calibrated to the actual level required by the role rather than to what sounds impressive. Culture and values questions that assess genuine alignment rather than the candidate's ability to identify and perform the expected answers.

For each candidate, it generates additional questions specific to their background — the things in their resume or prior interviews that warrant deeper examination before a decision is made.

## The Pipeline as a System

Hiring without pipeline visibility is hiring by feel. You know roughly how many people you have spoken to and roughly where the strong ones are in the process, but the picture is approximate enough that things fall through the gaps — a strong candidate who went cold because nobody followed up, an interview that was scheduled and then rescheduled and then forgotten, a reference check that was supposed to happen two weeks ago.

The skill maintains a complete pipeline for every open role. Every candidate tracked from first contact through hired or rejected, with their current stage, the date they entered it, the next action required, and who owns it. Candidates who have been sitting in a stage longer than expected are flagged before they go cold. Commitments made to candidates — to follow up, to schedule, to provide a decision — are tracked and surfaced before the deadline passes.

A candidate's experience of your hiring process is their first experience of your organization. Silence and disorganization communicate something about what it would be like to work there. The skill ensures the process reflects the organization you are trying to be.

## Communications That Respect Everyone's Time

Every interaction in a hiring process is a communication that either builds or erodes the candidate's view of your organization. The rejection that arrives six weeks after the final interview with a form letter. The offer that contains terms nobody discussed. The silence after a promising first conversation that leaves the candidate uncertain whether to continue their search.

The skill drafts every communication in the hiring process. Outreach to passive candidates that is specific enough to be worth reading. Interview invitations with everything the candidate needs to prepare. Rejection messages that are professional, warm, and specific enough to feel like a real response rather than an automated one. Offer letters that reflect everything discussed, leave nothing ambiguous, and make acceptance feel like the obvious next step.

Every draft is shown for approval before anything is sent. Nothing goes to a candidate without your review of the exact text.

## A Note on Fairness

Structured hiring processes are not just more predictive. They are more fair. When every candidate is assessed against the same criteria using the same questions, the decision is based on evidence rather than on how comfortable the interviewer felt in the conversation — a variable that correlates with similarity to the interviewer rather than with ability to do the job.

The skill is designed around this principle. The criteria are defined before the candidate is seen. The questions are prepared before the interview begins. The assessment is structured around evidence rather than impression. This does not eliminate judgment — judgment is required at every stage — but it anchors that judgment to something more reliable than gut feeling formed in the first five minutes.
